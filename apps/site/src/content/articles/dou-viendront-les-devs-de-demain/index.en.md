The previous chapter raised a question: in a contracting market, what justifies your place?

That’s a question for those who are already there. People with a job, experience, a network. But there’s a category of people for whom the question doesn’t even come up. Because they never had a place to begin with.

## Learn to Code

In 2013, Obama took part in the Hour of Code. He wrote a line of JavaScript in a Frozen tutorial. The message was simple: the future belongs to those who code. The American government asked for 4 billion dollars for computer science education.

The message got through. CS degrees doubled in ten years. From 51,000 to 112,000. Bootcamps exploded. Three months of training, a job promise, thousands of dollars in fees. An entire industry built on the idea that coding is the skill of the future.

And now the market is closing under their feet. Bootcamps are shutting down one after another. More than a dozen between 2023 and 2025. The institutions built to deliver on the promise are dying before the people they trained.

The irony: coding is the part AI does fastest.

## What’s Happening Now

Stanford analyzed the pay stubs of millions of workers. Employment for developers aged 22 to 25 has dropped 20% since late 2022. Those 30 and older, meanwhile, grew 6 to 12% over the same period.

The 15 biggest tech companies now hire only 7% new graduates. It was 14% before the pandemic. Cut in half.

The line that sums it all up comes from a senior engineer quoted by CIO.com: "Why hire a junior at $90,000 when GitHub Copilot costs $10 a month?"

37% of managers prefer using AI over hiring a junior. In India, 80% of engineering graduates have neither a job offer nor an internship.

This isn’t a local problem.

## Who the Buzz Serves

All of this is strategically incoherent. The AWS CEO himself says it: replacing juniors with AI is "one of the dumbest things I’ve heard." Juniors are the cheapest employees. They’re the best AI users. And without them, no one knows who takes over in ten years. Maybe we won’t need as many people. Maybe we will. Nobody really knows.

So why is everyone pushing in this direction?

Because the "AI replaces devs" narrative sells. It sells licenses, "prompt engineering" courses, board-level reorgs. The buzz isn’t a diagnosis. It’s a product.

MIT Technology Review interviewed more than 30 devs, executives, and researchers. Their conclusion: the productivity gains from AI may be illusory. Some are starting to wonder whether the expected results aren’t wildly overstated. The most rigorous study to date, conducted by METR in 2025 on experienced devs, measured that AI increased their completion time by 19%. Those same devs estimated they were saving 24%. The gap between what we believe and what we measure is enormous.

The disruption is real. But the gain is conditional on mastering the entire production chain with AI, and that chain is still incomplete and immature. Generated code produces 1.7 times more bugs than human-written code. The excitement is accelerating tool maturity, but it’s also fueling the race and the FOMO. 63% of IT decision-makers admit adopting AI out of fear of being left behind. That pressure of "if you don’t move now, you’re dead."

## Delegating Before You Know

There’s a deeper problem than the market.

Microsoft and Carnegie Mellon studied 319 workers. Those who trust AI the most are the ones who think least about what it produces. Critical thinking atrophying without anyone noticing.

A Dutch dev used AI every day for months. When he launched a side project without it, he found himself stuck on things that had been instinctive six months earlier. The moves he’d stopped practicing were gone.

And the detail that stings: Anthropic’s own engineers — the company building Claude — report that their skills are getting rusty.

It’s like short-form video and attention spans. When you get used to consuming information in 15-second fragments, reading deeply becomes an effort. It’s not that the tool is bad. It’s that if you’ve never built up your concentration, there’s nothing to delegate. You’re delegating a void.

Same mechanic with code. Except AI demands more reading than handwritten code. Your own code, you understand it by writing it. Generated code, you have to understand it by reading it. That’s harder, not easier.

The moment you validate without understanding, that’s where the erosion begins.

## Where Do Tomorrow’s Devs Come From?

The tasks we gave to juniors weren’t chores. Bug fixes, unit tests, CRUD, documentation. That was the learning curriculum. You learned the system by fixing it. You learned the architecture by adding a small piece on top. You learned reviews by getting reviewed.

If AI does that work, juniors never learn.

The organization becomes hollow. Heavy at the top, empty at the bottom. Mentorship disappears because the first reflex when you have a question isn’t to go find a colleague anymore. It’s to ask AI. Even at Anthropic, that’s what they’re observing.

If every company stops training juniors while counting on others to do it, nobody trains anybody. And in ten years, we’ll be fighting over the same aging devs and wondering where the shortage came from.

## The Future Gems

The data is bleak. But there’s something the numbers don’t capture.

Those who get through this period by building their critical thinking and their understanding of systems — while integrating AI into their learning — come out with a profile that will probably become the norm. Today, experienced devs have the advantage of the terrain. The moment they integrate AI into how they work, they stack what nobody else has yet.

Order matters. Understand first, delegate second. Not the other way around. Someone who delegates before building their foundations amplifies nothing. They’re automating a void.

For those just starting out, it’s territory nobody has mapped yet. No adapted curriculum, no standard path, no senior who’s been through it before them. But those who get through it — who learn how a system holds together before asking a machine to build it — they arrive at the same place. That expertise will surely become the next niche, the way assembly was when high-level languages arrived. Except that AI’s reach is so broad that it’s very hard to predict what that will really look like. It’s not a guarantee. But it’s a starting point nobody else has.
