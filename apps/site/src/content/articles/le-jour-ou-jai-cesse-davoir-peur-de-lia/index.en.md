Dev since 2011. Years of writing code, debugging systems at 3 a.m., explaining why "no, it doesn’t take five minutes".

Years of not asking too many questions. Dev was solid. There was work, there was demand.

And for the first time, I didn’t know where my craft was headed.

## The craft as I knew it

I started coding at 14. Not to "become a developer". For fun. To see if I could make things move on a screen.

When I made it my job, not much changed. I coded during the day, I coded in the evening. Side projects on weekends. A new tech to learn? Perfect, now I know what to do with my Saturday.

We were translators. You’d take a human need — "I want my customers to be able to order online" — and turn it into code. Line by line. Bug by bug. All-nighter by all-nighter.

And the best part? You could do all of it alone. Headphones on, screen in front of you, the outside world on mute. The introvert’s paradise.

## The day I hit the wall

My company was struggling. Financial trouble. I got sold the dream: "Now’s the time to go freelance, you’ve got the profile, go for it."

I went for it.

Overnight, no more colleagues. No more coffee machine to chat about an article you’d read. No one to say "did you see this thing?" When AI entered my daily life, I was alone in front of a screen. No team to put things in perspective, no buddy to compare experiences with. Just me and this question looping on repeat.

I wanted to use the change. Stop doing the same thing. Move from backend to frontend, then fullstack. A roadmap that made sense. I dove in.

AI? I’d seen the demos. Impressive, sure. Worrying, no. It was a gadget.

And then Claude 3.5 Sonnet arrived. Directly in the IDE. No more copy-pasting. AI that reads your code, understands your context, writes for you. Fluid. Natural.

That’s when I got it. This wasn’t a gadget anymore. And it cost nothing but a subscription.

## What I did with my fear

I could have denied it. A lot of people do. "AI doesn’t really understand." "It makes mistakes." "We’ll always need humans."

All of that is true. And all of that isn’t enough.

So I did what I always do when I don’t understand something: I dove in. I used AI every day. Not to test it. To _work_ with it.

And I discovered something counterintuitive.

## The revelation

The more I used AI, the more I understood what it couldn’t do. And that list isn’t technical. It’s deeply human.

**AI doesn’t know the invisible context.**

It can generate ten solutions. But it doesn’t know that the client says "yes" in the meeting and thinks "no" on the way out. That this shaky technical choice exists because it prevented a conflict between two teams. That the "technical debt" it’s proposing to clean up is actually a political compromise nobody dares reopen.

**AI has nothing to lose.**

When production goes down on a Sunday evening, you’re the one they call. Your reputation, your credibility, your next contract. AI doesn’t care. It has nothing to lose.

## What I do differently now

I always thought coder and architect were two sides of the same coin. The same job, two hats. Except before, the coder was eating up 80% of the time.

AI has shifted the balance. The time spent typing code is shrinking. What’s left is the part no one can do in my place: design, decide, anticipate.

Better design upfront, more robust code as output. AI didn’t eliminate my job. It freed me from its most mechanical part. But I can say that because I have more than ten years of code behind me. Years of mistakes that taught me to tell good solutions from bad ones. If I’d started with AI from day one, would I have that perspective? Honestly, I don’t know.

## And now?

Have I stopped being afraid? Not really. But the fear has changed shape.

Before, it was paralysis. The wall. Obsolescence.

Now it’s a strange mix of fear and excitement. Finding projects has never been this chaotic. Nobody knows what a dev is worth today. Or where to put the budget: AI licences, a senior, a junior to train. The rules change every six months.

But at least I’m no longer a spectator. I’m in the game.

We’re heading straight into the unknown. We might as well go in with our eyes open.

## Open questions

One question stays with me though. If AI frees me from the mechanical part, what’s stopping me from losing the muscle? Judgment is maintained through practice. When you stop reading every line, questioning every choice — are you really still a developer?

And what about people starting today? I’ve been coding since 2011. Years of mistakes, all-nighters, systems falling apart. That’s what gave me the perspective to judge what AI proposes. But a junior who starts with Copilot from day one — how do they develop that critical thinking? Judgment comes from experience. If you skip the experience, what exactly are you training?
