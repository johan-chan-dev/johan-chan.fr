## Clean code is only the surface

For a long time, I thought that’s what craft was: clean code. Well-named functions, classes with clear responsibilities, tests that document intent. Do that well, you’re a craftsman.

Then AI arrived. And it’s starting to know how to do all of that. Not always perfect on the first try, but the gap shrinks every day. If craft means writing clean code, then yes, it’s only a matter of time — with a simple subscription.

Clean code isn’t the craft. It’s a consequence of the care you put into the work, not the care itself.

Craft is what comes upstream. A carpenter, when they sculpt and shape, does it with a vision. A vision that comes from a need they understood before touching the wood. Same for us. Craft isn’t the quality of the code you produce — it’s the understanding that precedes it. "Does this code actually serve the people who use it and the ones who’ll maintain it?" That’s a question no AI asks.

## What makes the craftsman

If clean code is just a consequence, then what actually makes the craftsman? Thinking it through, I’ve identified three things — it’s definitely not exhaustive, and the lines between them are blurry. But these are the ones that come back to me most often: care, feedback, discipline.

### Care

We stop. We come back the next day, the week after, three months later. And then we have to find where we left off, understand what we did, and decide if it’s still the right direction.

That’s what care is. Not aesthetics. Survival. We’re human: we’re not always clear-headed, we go down the wrong path without realizing it. Care is giving yourself the means to pick up effectively and evaluate with fresh eyes.

Good naming, a clear structure, well-defined responsibilities — it’s not to look nice. It’s so that you in three months, or the next person who comes along, can pick up the work without rediscovering everything. Clean code is the result of that care. Not a checklist to follow — a choice you calibrate to the project.

### Feedback

The craftsman needs to know whether what they built holds up. Not in six months when it blows up in production. Now. Feedback is a fast signal that tells you whether you’re still on the right track.

Tests are the most obvious tool. You change something, you run the tests, you know in a few seconds if you broke anything. Without that, every change becomes a gamble. But feedback isn’t just tests. It’s deploying to production and observing what actually happens. It’s also showing the result to the person who asked for it, because we guess most of what we’re asked to do — and guessing isn’t understanding. Every feedback loop shortens the distance between what you think you did and what you actually did.

### Discipline

Discipline has two sides.

Resisting bad reflexes. We pile on layers "just in case," to protect against a risk that may never come. Faced with code we don’t understand, the reflex is to "fix" it: rename, refactor, rewrite. Fixing without understanding is the surest way to break something.

And forcing yourself into good habits. Read before you touch. Find the why before you change the how. Write the test you feel like skipping. Accept that if something seems off, maybe you just haven’t understood the context yet.

And behind all of it, pursue simplicity. Not simplicity as a starting point — as a direction. You always start with something fuzzy, too complex. And you work to make it simpler. Build for today, with the awareness that tomorrow will change things. Not armored against the future. *Open* to the future. Accept that "simple enough" is sufficient, and the rest is fear disguised as rigor.

It’s counterintuitive. It feels like you’re doing less. You’re actually doing better.

Through care, feedback, and discipline, you end up developing instinct. You sense when something’s off before you’ve even checked. But that instinct isn’t a pillar — it’s what emerges when you practice the rest.

## And AI in all of this?

Let’s be honest: AI is making progress on every one of these points. It can name things, structure things, test things, recognize patterns and improve consistency. The line moves every day.

But it risks nothing. It doesn’t have a project to maintain, no system whose choices it’ll have to own six months from now. The craftsman remains indispensable — not because they write better code, but because they carry the weight of what they build.

## Open questions

One question stays with me, which I’m deliberately leaving without a definitive answer.

**How do you develop craft without the practice?**

Care, discipline, instinct — all of it comes from practice. Years of reading code, getting things wrong, fixing without understanding and paying the price. That’s what forges the craftsman.

But if AI handles more and more of the production work, if a junior’s first instinct is to ask Copilot instead of figuring it out themselves, how do they build those muscles? Instinct doesn’t come from a tutorial. It comes from the mistakes you made and the time you spent understanding them.

You can’t shortcut experience. Or maybe you can, but differently. That’s a question I’m still sitting with.
