Craft is built through practice. Reading code, getting things wrong, paying the price for your mistakes. That’s what builds the muscle.

But there’s something we don’t see coming: you can also lose it.

## The better it gets, the less you look

AI-generated code improves every day. Suggestions are more relevant, patterns are cleaner, edge cases are handled better.

In theory, that’s good news. In practice, it creates a trap. The better the generated code gets, the more tempting it becomes to stop reading it. Why waste time checking something that works 95% of the time?

That’s exactly where craft starts to slip away. Not all at once. Slowly. So slowly you don’t even notice.

## The noise around it

And then there’s the noise. "I built a complete app in a single prompt." "An e-commerce site in 30 seconds." It’s like showing a machine where you feed in raw wood on one side and a finished piece of furniture comes out the other. One button, that’s it. Why would anyone need a carpenter anymore?

In reality, working with AI is a dialogue. You guide, you correct, you iterate. The first draft arrives faster, but the thinking that comes after still takes just as long. AI replaces the typing, not the thinking.

Except this narrative has real effects. For people already in the field, it reinforces the temptation to let go of the effort. For beginners, it’s discouraging: why spend years investing in a domain a machine can do better in 30 seconds? And for decision-makers, it plants the idea that an AI system will be enough. Why hire craftsmen when you can buy machines?

All of it leads to the same place. Less practice. And without practice, no craft.

## When you stop reading

There’s a moment when you flip. You go from "I check what AI gives me" to "I trust it." It’s not a conscious choice — it happens little by little, suggestion after suggestion.

When I re-read generated code, I’m doing several things at once. I check that it does what I want. I make sure it integrates with what’s already there. And above all, I maintain my ability to judge what’s good or bad.

That last part is what you let go of first. And it doesn’t start the day you stop reading — it starts the moment you read less. Every suggestion accepted without questioning it, every piece of code skimmed instead of really read. The judgment muscle atrophies like the others. And the problem isn’t letting go — it’s no longer knowing where it matters.

## Knowing where to look

Not all code deserves the same attention. Boilerplate, plumbing, format conversions. Skimming that is fine. The problem is you don’t always know in advance where the critical parts are hiding. That mundane utility function that’ll be called ten thousand times a second. That "temporary" abstraction that’ll shape the system for years. Those naming choices that’ll influence how the whole team thinks about the domain.

With experience, you develop an instinct for those zones. You sense when something deserves a pause. But that instinct has to evolve with AI. The critical zones shift. It’s no longer just "is this code clean?" — it’s "is this the right solution, or just the one that looked correct?"

For an experienced dev, that’s a recalibration. The reflex is there, it adapts. But for someone just starting out? That reflex comes from the mistakes you’ve made and the time you spent understanding them. If AI absorbs that friction, what does it get built on?

## What remains

The question isn’t "should you use AI?" It’s here, and it’s useful. The question is how to use it without losing what makes you good.

AI amplifies what you bring to it. If you bring discernment, it produces quality. If you bring laziness, it produces efficient mediocrity. Code that runs, but that’s worth nothing.

The good enough quality trap is confusing "it works" with "it’s good." AI can give you the first one easily. The second one, AI won’t do for you.
