Dev since 2011. Same chair. Same screen. Headphones on, the outside world on mute.

Except the job I do today isn’t the one I learned.

A year and a half ago, I panicked. I said it was because of AI, the market, obsolescence. Eleven chapters later, I don’t think that’s what it was. It went deeper.

I’ve always been the technician. The one you call when nothing works anymore. Family, friends, clients. "Ask Johan, he’ll know." My job wasn’t just coding. It was being the one who finds the solution. The resourceful jack-of-all-trades who gets the machine running again. That wasn’t what I did. It was who I was.

And when AI started solving in five minutes what would have taken me two hours, it wasn’t a colleague threatening me. It was my usefulness. My place. The feeling that nobody would need me anymore. That part of me was being absorbed by AI.

So I did what I always do when I don’t understand. I dove in. And after a while, I saw something I hadn’t seen at first.

AI proposes. It outputs the most probable solution based on what it knows. But it doesn’t guide. It needs someone who knows the terrain to tell whether what it’s proposing actually holds up. Like a GPS that doesn’t know everything. A closed road, a renamed address, a shortcut only locals know. It plows ahead confidently, and you’re the one who has to know something’s off.

And there’s something I’d almost forgotten. The technology is a means. Not an end. The job isn’t the code — it’s the need the code answers. Before, we’d build the solution as the need came into focus. Now, we can help sharpen the need itself. Understand someone else’s domain, because we have the technical command to translate what they’re saying into what needs to be built. We don’t produce faster — we explore wider. Because we spend less time wrestling with the solution and more time understanding the problem.

Nobody knows what this profession will look like in five years. Beck doesn’t know. Fowler doesn’t know. Neither do I. Maybe the disciplines I practice today will be obsolete. Maybe human judgment is the next thing to fall.

But even if all of that falls away, I’ll have learned to doubt what seems to be working. To sense that something’s off before knowing why. To not trust the first clean output.

And for those just discovering the profession: developing a sense of direction isn’t a luxury. You don’t need it every day. But it’s what lets you recognise that something’s wrong and that you need to start asking questions before going too far.

Software Craftsmanship, care in the code, the discipline of writing — that won’t disappear. As long as there are passionate people who want to understand every line, optimise an algorithm for the pleasure of it, know the internals of a language, it’ll exist. It’ll just have less space.

And alongside it, something is emerging. No name yet. A practice that comes after care in the code, where the focus is constraints and architecture in a complex system. Thinking the system, guiding AI, understanding the business problem, assembling it all into something coherent. It’s no less artisanal. It’s a different gesture.

The profession continues. The landscape has changed, and so has the approach to finding solutions. But the need for someone who understands the problem before looking for the answer — that’s still there.
