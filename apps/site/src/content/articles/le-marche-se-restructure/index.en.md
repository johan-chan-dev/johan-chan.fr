The previous chapter was about juniors. But it’s not just juniors. The entire market is shifting.

## Who’s cutting, and why

The restructurings are in the thousands. Block cut 4,000 positions — the CEO said it flat out: it’s AI. Microsoft, 15,000. Amazon, two successive waves: 14,000, then 16,000. In total, more than 245,000 tech layoffs worldwide in 2025, with 54,000 in the US citing AI as a factor.

Klarna was the textbook case. Headcount reduced from 5,500 to around 3,500 through attrition, with a target of 2,000, replacing positions with AI. Then a reversal: they’re rehiring. The U-turn is as much of a signal as the initial cut.

There’s one thing to keep in mind: causality isn’t always clear. AI is arriving at the same time as the post-2021-bubble correction. Companies that over-hired during COVID were already looking to cut. AI serves as a convenient justification for decisions that might have happened anyway. Both forces are acting simultaneously, and no one can say with certainty how much comes from one versus the other.

What’s certain: the narrative of "we’re replacing with AI" plays well with the board. It’s easier to sell than "we hired too many people." In tech, we call it AI washing — slapping the word AI on a decision to make it acceptable.

## The dev market by the numbers

On the job postings side, the decline is measurable. In the US, -35% compared to pre-COVID. In France, the slowdown is less pronounced, but the direction is the same.

And then there are the counter-signals. IBM is tripling its junior hires. The BLS maintains growth projections of +15% over ten years. CompTIA projects 371,000 new tech positions in 2025, including 156,000 in software engineering.

The market isn’t dead.

## It all depends on where you’re looking

The market is compressing and expanding at the same time. The same data, yet completely different realities depending on who you are and where you work.

### By role

Job postings for senior profiles increased 4% between 2024 and 2025. Those for juniors dropped 7%. In 2025, only 18% of tech postings in the US accept candidates with one year of experience or less.

Seniors who integrate AI combine field advantage with tool advantage. Their value goes up. Juniors — the previous chapter covered that.

And then there’s the segment nobody talks about: mid-level.

They have three to seven years of experience. Enough to be autonomous, not enough to have been through multiple tech cycles. Expensive enough that companies look hard at their role, not senior enough to be considered indispensable. When a company automates repetitive tasks, it’s their day-to-day that disappears first. When it invests in strategic profiles, that’s above them. The junior costs nothing. The senior is irreplaceable. The mid-level has to prove every single day that an agent can’t do their job.

It’s the blind spot in every analysis, and it’s probably the most exposed profile.

### By sector

Big tech is cutting. That’s the number you see everywhere. But at the same time, AI startups are absorbing $202 billion in investment in 2025. AI and ML hiring has jumped 88% in one year.

Traditional companies are accelerating their digital transition. Tech hiring up 23%. JPMorgan is actively recruiting software engineers and AI specialists. Most are just getting started. The work ahead is still enormous.

Consulting is cutting too. McKinsey plans to shed 10% of its workforce. Deloitte, EY, KPMG have gone through similar waves. Accenture is restructuring toward AI.

There are also the niches exploding. Cybersecurity has 4.8 million unfilled positions worldwide. The median salary in AI/ML exceeds $187,000.

The market isn’t contracting. It’s polarizing. Some sectors are losing positions that others are creating, but not in the same places or for the same profiles.

### By geography

Silicon Valley has lost 80,000 tech jobs in two years. But since mid-2024, Nvidia, Meta, and Apple have recreated 15,000 positions there. The Valley is emptying and refilling at the same time, but not with the same people. The eliminated positions were generalist; the ones being created require AI specialization.

In France, IT startups have created net jobs since the start of 2024. Developers remain among the most sought-after profiles. The slowdown is there, but it’s not the same scale as in the US.

In India, it’s brutal. The four biggest IT exporters (TCS, Infosys, Wipro, HCL) went from 225,000 to 60,000 graduate hires in one year. Minus 73%. The Indian market is absorbing the shock first because it was the most exposed to automatable tasks.

Europe is short 10 million digital specialists to meet its 2030 objectives. The European market is in shortage while others are laying off. Same job, not the same reality.

## What the numbers don’t say on their own

The tool that was supposed to free up time hasn’t delivered on its promise. Expectations rise with velocity. You produce faster, you’re asked for more. The net gain is close to zero.

And confidence in AI tools themselves is declining. From 40% to 29% in one year. Overall developer favorability toward AI has dropped from 72% to 60%. Those who use AI daily feel more secure in their jobs, but they’re also the ones who measure most clearly the gap between the promise and the reality.

## A terrain nobody controls

Everyone has ideas. But nothing has time to mature. The tool changes shape faster than practices can stabilize. Some companies place small, cautious bets. Others go all in. Either way, it’s a gamble. And execution doesn’t depend on AI itself, but on how you plug it into what already exists. Today’s guides will be obsolete tomorrow. Until a real discipline emerges, the market will keep on lurching.

When the delivery process keeps changing shape, what does that do to the quality of what we ship?
